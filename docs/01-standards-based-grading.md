# What is standards-based grading?

*This note summarizes my (Camelia Karimianpour) ideas and work in implementing SBG in a large firts year math course. Section 2 describes the
real course this template distills. Citations are at the end.*

## The idea

Traditional grading assigns a single mark to a complex piece of work and
aggregates those marks into a course grade. Mastery-based approaches
instead grade against explicit learning objectives. The tradition goes
back to Bloom's "learning for mastery" (1968) [2], which tested the
assumption that most students can learn given enough time and feedback.

**Standards-based grading (SBG)** is the variant in which the objectives
are written as a list of *standards* — concrete, student-facing
statements, often action-framed ("I can ...") — and student work is
assessed for *mastery of standards*, not for points. Campbell, Clark &
O'Shaughnessy (2020) [4] give the defining pillars, which this template's
documentation follows:

1. a clear list of objectives or standards exists;
2. assessment is for mastery, not points;
3. students get multiple opportunities (reattempts) to demonstrate mastery;
4. only eventual mastery matters.

Karimianpour (CMS 2025 talk) adds an explicit fifth element: SBG is *a
feedback system* — the point of the records is communication with
students, not just bookkeeping.

## Why instructors adopt it

Recurring motivations in the literature and in the MAT188 materials:
grades vs. learning (students optimize for points rather than
understanding [5]), the difficulty of interpreting a single aggregate
grade, the mental-health cost of high-stakes exams, and the wish to give
effective, actionable feedback. Feldman [7] argues such "veil-lifting"
grading practices are also more equitable. Surveys of implementations in
mathematics include Elsinger & Lewis [6] (SBG frameworks across lower-level
courses), Harsy [8] (variations in mastery-based testing), and Zimmerman
[12] (physics).

## Why instructors avoid it (honestly)

Documented costs from the MAT188 experience [CEEA paper]: substantial
pre-course design work (standards, question banks, rubrics, TA training);
in-term administrative load (tracking, resubmissions, report generation —
the reason this template's pipeline exists); student and TA buy-in
("students only see the final product" [9]; in a 2022 survey, 67% of the
MAT188 cohort still said they preferred conventional rubric grading);
and the risk that students misread "standards demonstrated / standards
covered" as a conventional percentage grade, which requires sustained
communication to prevent.

## The space in between ("hyphenated grading")

Pure SBG would mean no exams and unlimited regrading; in a large
coordinated course neither is practical. Karimianpour's "hyphenated
grading" framing (CMS 2025): there is always a space between traditional
and pure-mastery grading, defined by you, your students, and your
institution's constraints — start with a low-stakes corner of the course,
then refine and scale up. In MAT188, SBG governed tutorials, online
homework, and gateway quizzes, while
midterms and the final remained summative; exam questions were still
*labeled* with standards so reports could give feedback either way.

Suggested adoption road map: design LSs ->
identify your space in between -> create questions and examples for LSs ->
try hyphenated grading in a low-stakes corner -> refine and gradually
scale up. Practical advice from the same talk: beware self-induced exams
(reassessment events that recreate exam pressure), confusion causes harm,
one thing at a time.
